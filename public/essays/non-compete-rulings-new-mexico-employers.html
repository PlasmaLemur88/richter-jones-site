What the new non-compete rulings actually mean for New Mexico employers

Three recent decisions have redrawn the line between protecting trade secrets and restraining trade. What employers and employees need to know before the next hire.

Jeremy R. Jones March 27, 2026 11 min read

Non-compete agreements have always existed in a kind of legal twilight in New Mexico. They are not prohibited. They are not presumptively valid. They occupy a narrow corridor of enforceability that depends almost entirely on how carefully the drafter respected the boundaries — and for years, most drafters did not.

Three decisions in the last eighteen months have changed the terrain. Not dramatically, not with the sweep of a legislative ban, but with the quiet precision of courts that have grown tired of reading overbroad restrictions dressed up as trade-secret protection. If you employ people in New Mexico and your offer letters contain a non-compete, you need to understand what these rulings actually did.

The old standard and why it stopped working

New Mexico has long applied a reasonableness test to non-competes: the restriction must be no broader than necessary to protect a legitimate business interest, limited in time, limited in geography, and not unduly burdensome on the employee. This is textbook law. Every state with enforceable non-competes uses some version of it.

The problem was never the standard. The problem was that employers treated it as a ceiling rather than a floor. A two-year, statewide restriction on an employee who worked in one office in one city was “reasonable” on paper because the company did business statewide. Courts accepted this logic for a long time. The recent decisions stopped accepting it.

The question is no longer whether the restriction is reasonable in the abstract. The question is whether the restriction is reasonable for this employee, doing this job, with access to this information.

This is a significant shift. It means you can no longer use a standard non-compete clause across your entire workforce. The sales director who knows your pricing model and the junior analyst who updates spreadsheets cannot be bound by the same language.

Non-competes versus non-solicitation agreements

The distinction matters more now than it used to. A non-compete says: you cannot work for a competitor. A non-solicitation agreement says: you cannot take our clients or recruit our employees when you leave. Courts have consistently been more willing to enforce the latter.

The reason is intuitive. A non-solicitation agreement protects specific, identifiable relationships that the employer invested in building. A non-compete protects market position, which is a different thing — closer to restraint of trade than protection of property. The recent rulings have made this distinction sharper. If your goal is to prevent a departing employee from walking out the door with your client list, a well-drafted non-solicitation agreement will do the job. A non-compete will try to do more and may accomplish less.

What to do before the next offer letter

If you are an employer in New Mexico, the practical advice is straightforward. First, stop using a single non-compete clause for all employees. Tailor the restriction to the role. Second, consider whether a non-solicitation agreement achieves what you actually need. Third, make sure the duration and geographic scope are defensible — not theoretically defensible, but defensible for the specific person signing the document.

If you are an employee who signed a non-compete in the last few years, the landscape has shifted in your favor. That does not mean the agreement is unenforceable. It means the burden on the employer to justify enforcement has gotten heavier, and the courts are reading these clauses with less deference than they once did.

The non-compete is not dead in New Mexico. But the era of the boilerplate non-compete — the same clause stamped into every offer letter regardless of role, seniority, or access to confidential information — is over. The employers who adjust will protect what matters. The ones who don’t will discover that a clause nobody can enforce is worse than no clause at all.
